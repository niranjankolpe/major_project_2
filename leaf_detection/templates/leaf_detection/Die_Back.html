DIE BACK

Management

  • Avoid mechanical injuries to the plants.
  • Prevent termites and longicorn beetles causing damage to the plants.

  • Reduce plant stress, including stress caused by drought and nutritional deficiencies

  • Reduce sunburn of the trunk and branches when severely pruning the trees.

  • Prune dying branches by cutting the branch back to below the edge of the dead or symptomatic areas. Disinfect the pruning tool after each plant. Remove the symptomatic pruned branches from the orchard.

  • Avoid pruning mango trees when the canopy is wet or during rainfall

  • Follow the ‘come clean go clean’ practices, and make sure to clean and disinfect any tree pruning machinery when moving it between farms.

  • Although mango mulch is an important source of nutrients, removal of under tree mango mulch/litter in orchards with high disease load may be beneficial as it is a potential source of inoculum.

  • There is no fungicide currently registered in Australia to control mango common dieback.

Monitoring

  • Regular Inspections: Conduct routine inspections of your mango trees to look for any signs of dieback. Check for wilting leaves, dead or dying branches, and overall poor health of the tree.

  • Identify the Cause: Determine the underlying cause of dieback. This could include fungal infections like anthracnose or powdery mildew, nutrient deficiencies (such as potassium or magnesium), water stress, root rot, or insect infestations.

  • Soil and Tissue Testing: Conduct soil tests to assess nutrient levels and pH. Also, consider leaf tissue analysis to identify any specific nutrient deficiencies. Correct any imbalances through appropriate fertilization.

  • Proper Irrigation: Ensure your mango trees receive adequate water, especially during dry periods. However, avoid overwatering, as waterlogged soil can lead to root rot and exacerbate dieback.

  • Fungicide Treatments: If fungal infections are identified as the cause of dieback, apply appropriate fungicides according to label instructions. Copper-based fungicides are commonly used to control fungal diseases in mango trees

  • Pest Management: Implement pest control measures to prevent insect infestations, which can weaken the tree and make it more susceptible to diseases like dieback.

Direct Control

  • Pruning: Remove and dispose of dead or infected branches to prevent the spread of disease.

  • Watering: Ensure proper watering to avoid water stress. Use a drip irrigation system to keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged.

  • Mulching: Apply organic mulch around the base of the tree to retain moisture and regulate soil temperature.
