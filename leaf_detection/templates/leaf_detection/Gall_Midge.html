GALL MIDGE

Management

  • The surviving stages of midges in fallen leaves should be collected and destroyed.

  • Hoeing should be done under the tree canopy of mango tree followed by irrigation to kill the pupating midges in soil.

  • After fruit harvesting pruning should be done properly and damaged branches are burned away from orchard.

  • Plastic sheet is used to break life cycle of midge under the plant canopy to cover soil. It prevents emergence of adult from soil and also prevent dropping larvae to go into soil for pupation that results population decreases.

  • Use of foliar application of 2 % Neem extract on affected mango trees

  • Soil treatments have also been used to kill carry over population of larvae in soil under mango trees, apply through drenching chlorpyrifos (e.g. Lorsben 500ml/100L water) OR apply fipronil (e.g. Regent/Refree 8 kg/acre) under the canopy of trees.

Monitoring

  • Use of Traps: Some traps are specifically designed to attract and capture gall midges. These traps usually contain substances that mimic the scent of host plants, attracting the midges. Check the traps regularly for any captured insects.

  • Sampling: Randomly collect samples of mango leaves from different parts of the tree and inspect them under a magnifying glass for the presence of gall midge larvae or eggs. This method helps in detecting infestations at an early stage.

  • Integrated Pest Management (IPM): Implement an IPM approach, which combines various pest control methods, including cultural, biological, and chemical control strategies, to manage gall midge infestations effectively while minimizing environmental impact.

  • Yellow Sticky Traps: Yellow sticky traps are particularly effective in capturing gall midges, as these insects are attracted to the color yellow. Hang yellow sticky traps in the mango orchard and check them periodically.

Direct Control

  • Sanitation: Keep the orchard clean by removing and destroying infested plant parts. This reduces the population of gall midges.

  • Pruning: Prune affected branches to remove the galls and destroy them to prevent further spread.

  • Crop Rotation: If possible, avoid planting mango trees in areas where gall midges have been a recurring problem.

  • Predators and Parasitoids: Encourage natural enemies of gall midges, such as predatory insects and parasitic wasps, by maintaining a diverse ecosystem in the orchard. Avoid using broad-spectrum pesticides that may harm these beneficial insects.

  • Insecticides: If cultural and biological control methods are insufficient, consider using insecticides as a last resort. Consult with local agricultural authorities or entomologists to determine the most effective and least harmful insecticides for controlling gall midges on mango trees. Follow all label instructions and safety precautions when applying pesticides.
